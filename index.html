Illustrative Operating Model  ·  For Discussion with SBI Card

Proposed Operating Construct for FD-backed SBIC Credit Card

Addressing the structural challenges of separating FD issuance and credit card issuance across entities — using KVB as an illustrative example.

Hyperface Technologies · June 2026 · Private and Confidential

The Problem

SBI Card's setup is structurally different from existing market examples

Current FD-backed secured cards (Super.Money, PhonePe Secured Card) work because one bank manages FD issuance, lien marking, and card issuance. SBI Card's proposed construct separates these — creating three challenges without off-the-shelf answers.

Market standard:  FD issuer = Card issuer  (same bank)
vs
SBI Card's context:  FD issuer  ≠  Card issuer  (separate entities)

Challenge 01

KYC Ownership and Reliance

Two separately regulated entities. The extent to which an NBFC-CC can rely on a bank's KYC under the RBI KYC Master DirectionS2 is not established for a non-cobrand multi-entity structure.

Challenge 02

Cross-entity Collateral Enforceability

Standard bank lien applies only when the same bank holds the deposit and the credit relationship. SBI Card does not hold KVB's deposit relationship — a different legal mechanism is required.

Challenge 03

Seamless Customer Onboarding

The customer must interact with two separately regulated entities across one continuous journey — without experiencing it as two separate applications.

Structures Under Evaluation

Two potential operating structures

Both structures share the same core challenges. They differ in how tractably those challenges can be addressed.

Our View

Recommendation

Proposed Approach

Option 2 for the initial pilot — designed with Option 1 extensibility in view

Both options are directionally workable. They differ not in whether the core challenges exist, but in how tractably they can be addressed. Option 2 is the lower-friction path to a validated construct.

Addresses the hardest problem more tractably

Cross-entity collateral requires KVB to commit to SBI Card. In Option 2, this can be embedded in the existing cobrand agreement. In Option 1 it must be constructed from scratch with no commercial backbone.

More defensible KYC reliance basis

The cobrand relationship creates a defined commercial context for KYC sharing. Neither option resolves the NBFC-CC regulatory question — but Option 2 provides a more substantive commercial foundation.

Simpler journey reduces pilot risk

No mid-journey partner-bank selection. Fewer decision points means lower drop-off and a cleaner experience to validate.

Better regulatory fit

An FD-backed cobrand card can be anchored in the RBI cobrand card framework (Master Direction, 2022)S3. Option 1 is less standard and may require additional regulatory engagement.

Pilot learnings transfer to Option 1

KYC reliance model, collateral framework, and default handling validated under Option 2 are directionally applicable to Option 1 when SBI Card expands to a multi-bank model.

Note on long-term architecture: Option 1 remains better suited to SBI Card's strategic interest in a multi-bank FD network. This is a pilot recommendation — the construct should be designed with Option 1 extensibility in view from the start.

Conditions on this recommendation

  • Existing KVB cobrand agreement requires material amendment to cover FD-backed card specifics, pledge arrangements, and invocation procedures — this is a precondition, not a routine change
  • Cross-entity collateral mechanism requires independent legal validation and is not assumed to be resolved
  • KYC reliance scope requires SBI Card compliance confirmation — Option 2 is directionally simpler, not regulatory-cleared
  • KVB's technical readiness for API-based FD confirmation and pledge marking requires validation before pilot Assumed capable

The Hard Part

The collateral construct

Standard bank lien mechanics don't apply cross-entity. A pledge / tripartite arrangement is required — and it's the most structurally novel element of this construct.

Why a standard bank lien doesn't work here

A bank can only exercise its right of lien over deposits held with itself. SBI Card is not KVB's depositor-creditor — so SBI Card cannot simply "lien-mark" a KVB FD. A different legal mechanism is needed.

How the proposed pledge / tripartite structure works Proposed — subject to legal validation

1

Customer opens an FD at KVB

The FD is in the customer's name. KVB holds the funds. By default, only KVB can act on it — SBI Card has no standing here.

Customer KVB
2

Customer pledges the FD in favour of SBI Card

The customer signs a pledge document giving SBI Card the legal right to instruct KVB to break the FD in the event of a credit card default. The customer cannot withdraw or close the FD while the pledge is active.

Customer signs SBI Card is beneficiary
3

KVB acknowledges the pledge and commits to honour it

KVB signs a tripartite agreement: it marks the FD as restricted and agrees that if SBI Card sends an invocation instruction, KVB will break the FD and remit proceeds. In Option 2, this is embedded in the cobrand agreement. In Option 1, it must be negotiated from scratch with each partner bank.

KVB commits

Illustrative legal mechanisms — subject to independent legal review

Pledge of FD Receipt (FDR)

Customer pledges the FD certificate to SBI Card. KVB acknowledges and agrees to honour invocation instructions.

Tripartite Agreement

Three-party arrangement between Customer, KVB, and SBI Card. KVB contractually commits to FD closure and remittance on SBI Card's request.

Assignment of Proceeds

Customer assigns the right to FD proceeds to SBI Card for the duration of the arrangement.

Specific legal mechanism to be determined through independent legal review. Enforceability under Indian law — including in insolvency or dispute scenarios — must be confirmed before this construct is progressed. This document does not constitute legal advice.

Default Handling

Lien invocation and default handling

The invocation process is triggered by SBI Card and executed by KVB. Net proceeds after deductions are remitted to SBI Card; any residual outstanding follows standard recovery.

Invocation flow

1

Customer crosses agreed delinquency threshold

SBI Card initiates customer communication and allows a cure period. Invocation does not proceed until the cure period has expired without resolution.

SBI Card
2

SBI Card raises invocation request to KVB

On expiry of cure period without resolution, SBI Card sends a formal invocation instruction to KVB, referencing the pledge and the outstanding dues.

SBI Card KVB receives
3

KVB forecloses the FD and calculates net proceeds

KVB breaks the FD, deducts applicable TDS and premature closure penalty, and calculates net proceeds available for remittance. Net proceeds will be below FD face value.

KVB
4

KVB remits proceeds; residual follows SBI Card recovery

KVB remits net proceeds to SBI Card within the agreed SLA. If outstanding dues exceed net proceeds, the residual balance follows SBI Card's standard recovery process.

KVB SBI Card
