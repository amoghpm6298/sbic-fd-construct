How to build an FD-backed SBI Card credit card when the FD and the card come from two different companies

Most secured cards work because one bank holds your Fixed Deposit (FD) and also issues your credit card. Here, KVB holds the FD and SBI Card issues the credit card — two separate regulated entities. This document explains the challenges that creates, and how they can be solved.

Hyperface Technologies · June 2026 · Private and Confidential

The Problem

SBI Card's setup is different from every other secured card in the market

Other FD-backed secured cards — like Super.Money and PhonePe Secured Card — work because one bank does everything: it holds your Fixed Deposit and issues the credit card. Here, KVB holds the FD and SBI Card issues the card. That separation creates three challenges that have no ready-made solution.

How the market works today:  The same bank holds your FD and issues your credit card
vs
This construct:  KVB holds the FD  ·  SBI Card issues the credit card  (two different companies)

Challenge 01

Who verifies the customer's identity — and can one company rely on the other's work?

KVB will verify the customer's identity (KYC) when opening the FD. SBI Card, which is separately regulated as a card-issuing company, may need to rely on that verification rather than starting from scratch. But how much SBI Card can legally rely on KVB's KYC — without doing its own full check — isn't clearly defined in RBI guidelines for this type of arrangement.S2

Challenge 02

How can SBI Card use the FD as security if it doesn't hold it?

Normally, if a customer stops paying, a bank can freeze or break their Fixed Deposit to recover the dues — this right is called a lien. It only works when the same bank holds both the deposit and the credit. Here, KVB holds the FD but SBI Card has the credit relationship. SBI Card cannot simply freeze a KVB deposit — a different legal arrangement is needed.

Challenge 03

Making it feel like one application, not two

The customer needs to open an FD with KVB and apply for a credit card with SBI Card — two separate companies with separate regulated processes. The goal is to make this feel like one smooth, connected experience rather than two separate applications.

Structures Under Evaluation

Two ways to structure this

Both options have the same underlying challenges. The difference is how easily — and how cheaply — those challenges can be resolved in practice.

Our View

Recommendation

Proposed Approach

Option 2 for the initial pilot — designed with Option 1 extensibility in view

Both options can work. The challenges are the same in both — what differs is how hard each challenge is to solve. Option 2 is the simpler path to proving this concept works.

Solves the hardest problem more easily

Getting KVB to formally commit to acting on SBI Card's instructions (breaking the FD if the customer defaults) is the hardest part. In Option 2, this commitment can be added to the existing cobrand agreement between KVB and SBI Card. In Option 1, an entirely new legal arrangement must be negotiated from scratch.

Stronger basis for sharing customer identity data

Because KVB and SBI Card already have a cobrand relationship, there's a more credible legal basis for KVB to share customer identity information with SBI Card. Neither option fully resolves the regulatory question — but Option 2 has stronger footing going in.

Simpler experience means fewer customers drop off

The customer never has to choose a bank mid-application. Fewer decision points = lower drop-off and a cleaner experience to test and learn from.

Closer fit with existing RBI rules

An FD-backed co-branded card can be anchored within RBI's existing framework for co-branded credit cards (2022)S3. Option 1 sits outside existing frameworks and would need more regulatory groundwork.

Pilot learnings apply to Option 1 later

Everything validated in the Option 2 pilot — how identity sharing works, how the FD security arrangement holds up, how defaults are handled — can be applied when SBI Card expands to a multi-bank model under Option 1.

Long-term view: Option 1 — where SBI Card works with multiple FD banks, not just KVB — is the better fit for SBI Card's long-term goal of a broad FD network. This recommendation is for the pilot only. The design should be built from the start with Option 1 expansion in mind, so it doesn't need to be rebuilt later.

Conditions on this recommendation

  • The existing KVB–SBI Card cobrand agreement must be significantly updated to cover FD-backed card terms, the pledge arrangement, and what happens if a customer defaults. This is a major legal change — not a paperwork update.
  • The legal arrangement for using the FD as security across two separate entities requires independent legal review. It cannot be assumed to be straightforward.
  • SBI Card's compliance team must formally confirm exactly how much of KVB's identity verification it can rely on. Option 2 is simpler — but it's not yet regulatory-approved.
  • KVB must be technically capable of confirming FD creation and marking pledges via API (automated data connections). This needs to be tested before the pilot begins. Assumed capable

The Hard Part

How SBI Card secures itself against the KVB Fixed Deposit

This is the most structurally novel part of the whole construct. Normally, a bank that holds both your deposit and your credit can simply freeze the deposit if you stop paying — this right is called a lien. Here, the deposit is at KVB but the credit is with SBI Card. A different legal arrangement — a pledge — is needed.

Why the standard approach doesn't apply here

A bank's right to freeze a deposit (called a lien) only works when it holds both the deposit and the outstanding debt. SBI Card is the creditor — but it doesn't hold the FD. KVB does. So SBI Card has no automatic right over a KVB deposit. A separate legal arrangement is needed where the customer formally gives SBI Card this right.

Three possible legal structures — all need independent legal review before use

Pledge of FD Certificate

The customer pledges their FD certificate to SBI Card as security. KVB acknowledges this and agrees to break the FD and send proceeds to SBI Card if requested due to a default.

Tripartite Agreement

A legal contract signed by all three parties — Customer, KVB, and SBI Card. KVB formally commits to breaking the FD and sending the money to SBI Card upon request.

Assignment of Proceeds

The customer transfers their right to the FD's proceeds over to SBI Card for the duration of the credit card arrangement.

Specific legal mechanism to be determined through independent legal review. Enforceability under Indian law — including in insolvency or dispute scenarios — must be confirmed before this construct is progressed. This document does not constitute legal advice.

Default Handling

What happens when a customer stops paying

If the customer defaults on their credit card, SBI Card instructs KVB to break the FD and recover the dues. KVB deducts applicable taxes and penalties, then sends the remaining amount to SBI Card. If the FD doesn't cover the full outstanding amount, SBI Card uses its standard collections process for the rest.

Step-by-step: how the FD is used to recover dues

1

Customer misses payments beyond the agreed limit

SBI Card contacts the customer and gives them a window to pay (a "cure period") before taking any action on the FD. The FD is not touched until this window closes without resolution.

SBI Card
2

SBI Card formally instructs KVB to break the FD

If the customer hasn't resolved the overdue amount by the deadline, SBI Card sends KVB a formal request to break the FD, specifying the outstanding amount owed.

SBI Card KVB receives
3

KVB breaks the FD and calculates what's available after deductions

KVB closes the FD early, deducting applicable tax (TDS) and the penalty for premature closure. The amount sent to SBI Card will be less than the original FD value because of these deductions.

KVB
4

KVB sends the funds to SBI Card; any remaining dues go to collections

KVB transfers the net amount to SBI Card within an agreed timeframe. If the FD proceeds don't fully cover what the customer owes, SBI Card uses its standard collections process to recover the rest.

KVB SBI Card

Regulatory

Regulatory questions that must be answered before launch

These are the key regulatory questions that must be formally answered before this construct can go live. All of them need sign-off from SBI Card and KVB's compliance teams, and in several cases from external lawyers. This is not a regulatory opinion — it's a checklist of what needs to be confirmed.

Area Key question for validation
How much of KVB's identity verification can SBI Card rely on? SBI Card is separately regulated and may need to verify certain things about the customer independently. How much it can rely on KVB's KYC — and what SBI Card must still check itself — needs to be confirmed under RBI guidelines.S2
Is the pledge legally enforceable across two separate regulated entities? The pledge gives SBI Card the right to instruct KVB to break the FD. Whether this holds up legally — especially in a dispute or insolvency scenario — needs independent legal review. In Option 2, the cobrand agreement is assumed to be the legal foundation for KVB's commitment. Assumed in Option 2
What must the customer be told, and does the consent meet privacy law? RBI credit card rules specify what must be disclosed to the customer. India's data privacy law (DPDP Act, 2023)S1 governs how identity data can be shared across companies — including that consent must be specific to its purpose and customers must be able to withdraw it.
Does this FD-backed cobrand structure comply with RBI's cobrand card rules? (Option 2) RBI has specific rules for co-branded credit cards (2022)S3, including what a cobrand partner (KVB) is allowed to do. This construct needs to be validated against those rules.
Is Hyperface's role compliant with RBI's IT outsourcing rules? Hyperface connecting KVB and SBI Card's systems counts as IT outsourcing under RBI rules (2023).S4 Both KVB and SBI Card must ensure they stay in full control of their regulated activities and can operate without Hyperface if needed.
Who handles customer complaints that involve both KVB and SBI Card? If a customer has a complaint about both the FD and the credit card, who resolves it — and through which channel? It must also be confirmed whether RBI's Ombudsman covers disputes in a cross-entity arrangement like this. This must be defined before launch.
Option 2 is simpler from a regulatory angle — but "simpler" doesn't mean "approved". Every question above still needs formal sign-off. Nothing here should be assumed resolved.

Q2

How much of KVB's identity verification can SBI Card legally rely onS2, and what must SBI Card still verify independently?

Q3

Which legal mechanism should be used to give SBI Card rights over the KVB FD — pledge, assignment, or three-party agreement — and how does SBI Card plan to get it independently reviewed?

Q4
